1. Overview

Metabolomics is the profiling of small molecules (metabolites) in complex biological matrices. The Frezza Lab is equipped with Thermo Scientific Orbitrap Exploris 120 and 240 mass spectrometers coupled to a Vanquish Horizon UHPLC system. We focus on primary, water‑soluble metabolites essential for growth, development, and reproduction. An in‑house metabolite library (including compounds from IROA databases) is used in a semi‑targeted approach to investigate metabolic pathways.

2. Experimental Design

Success in metabolomics starts with designing the right experiment. After an initial collaboration inquiry, a discussion is scheduled with Prof. Christian Frezza and the team to understand the project background, study aims, and to determine the best strategies and methods. Please provide a brief project summary (via PowerPoint or Word) and any preliminary data to help tailor the experimental approach.

3. Sample Preparation and Submission

Prior to analysis, a preliminary sample submission form must be completed and sent to us. Detailed protocols are provided for different sample types:

  • Cultured Cells: Cells should be washed rapidly with PBS, extracted with 500 µl extraction buffer per 106 cells, and processed promptly. Autosampler vials must be labeled with researcher initials, experiment number, and sample number.
  • Cell Culture Media (CCM): Centrifuge media to remove cells, mix with extraction solution, and prepare a pooled QC sample.
  • Tissues: Tissues should be excised quickly, snap‑frozen, homogenized (using Precellys tubes), and extracted using 25 µl extraction solution per mg tissue.
  • Biofluids: Blood samples (plasma/serum) must be processed promptly with recommended anticoagulants and may require an additional filtration step.

4. Data Acquisition

Data acquisition is performed using hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) combined with high-resolution mass spectrometry. Pooled QC samples are analyzed at regular intervals (every 6–8 injections) to monitor instrument performance and ensure data quality. Both full‑scan MS and MS2 fragmentation methods are used, depending on the experimental requirements.

5. Data Processing

Metabolite signal intensities are provided as relative quantifications. Data processing includes:

  • Normalization of raw signal intensities.
  • Natural abundance correction for tracer‑labelled studies.
  • Generation of visualizations such as PCA plots, heat maps, bar graphs, and volcano plots.

6. Biological Interpretation & Publication

Collaborators work closely with our team to interpret metabolic changes and support manuscript preparation. We discuss results in detail and, if needed, perform additional experiments until robust conclusions can be drawn.

7. Experimental Design Considerations

Considerations include the choice of biological, technical, and analytical replicates. For cell culture studies, at least five technical replicates per condition are recommended, repeated on different days. In animal studies, at least four biological replicates are preferred.

8. Stable Isotope Tracing

Stable isotope tracers (e.g., 13C, 15N, 2H) allow the tracking of nutrient conversion into metabolites. Care must be taken to minimize metabolic perturbation when introducing tracers. Correction for natural abundance is performed to distinguish labelled from naturally occurring isotopes.

9. Sample Replication

Replication is key to ensuring robust data. Biological replicates should be from independent sources, and technical replicates (repeating the complete procedure) help assess experimental variance. Pilot experiments may use fewer replicates.

10. Sample Preparation Considerations

Special considerations for sample preparation include:

  • Cultured Cells: Use consistent cell confluency, rapid quenching, and standardized extraction protocols.
  • Media: Include cell‑free controls and monitor for changes in nutrient consumption and metabolite release.
  • Tissues: Use snap‑freezing or freeze‑clamping to minimize post‑excision metabolic changes.
  • Biofluids: Process samples promptly to avoid artefacts from cell metabolism and repeated freeze/thaw cycles.

11. Quantitation

Quantitation is generally provided as relative signal intensities. For absolute quantification, an internal standard serial dilution approach is used. Results for tissues, biofluids, and cellular samples are normalized differently (e.g., per mg tissue, per volume, or per cell count).

12. LC-MS Limitations

LC-MS can detect hundreds to thousands of metabolites with high sensitivity, but it is not highly precise. Ionization efficiency varies widely among compounds, and isobaric interferences can complicate peak annotation. For critical results, further validation with isotopic internal standards or alternative methods is advised.

13. Data Analysis Overview

Our data analysis workflow includes:

  • Primary Data Reporting: Providing peak area data with accompanying metabolite metadata.
  • PCA Plots: To assess similarities and identify outliers.
  • Heat Maps: To visualize overall metabolite patterns.
  • Bar Graphs & Volcano Plots: For illustrating significant metabolic changes.
  • Tracer-labelled Data: Represented as absolute ion magnitude or fractional intensity, often visualized with stacked bar graphs.

14. Protocols & Materials

Materials include LC‑MS grade Methanol, Acetonitrile, ultrapure water, and standards (e.g., Valine‑d8). Additional materials include Eppendorf tubes, cell scrapers, autosampler vials, cryo‑labels, and dry ice.

Extraction Protocols

A. Cultured Cells:

1. Count cells and calculate the extraction buffer (typically 500 µl per 106 cells).
2. Remove medium, wash quickly with PBS, and aspirate thoroughly.
3. Place plate on dry ice; add extraction buffer and incubate on a dry ice‑methanol bath for 20 minutes.
4. Scrape cells, transfer to pre‑chilled tubes, shake in a Thermomixer at 4°C for 15 minutes, and centrifuge. Collect the top 80% of the supernatant.
5. Prepare a pooled sample from equal aliquots of each sample.

B. Cell Culture Media (CCM):

1. Centrifuge 80 µl of CCM to remove cells.
2. Add supernatant to 350 µl of extraction solution on dry ice.
3. Mix, shake at 4°C for 15 minutes, and centrifuge. Collect the top 80% of the supernatant.
4. Pool equal volumes for a QC sample.

C. Tissues:

1. Weigh and label tissue (10–20 mg recommended).
2. Add extraction solution at 25 µl per mg tissue.
3. Homogenize using a Precellys tissue homogenizer.
4. Shake, centrifuge, and collect the supernatant carefully.
5. Prepare a pooled sample.

D. Plasma/Serum:

1. Collect blood into a heparin tube (or allow serum to clot).
2. Centrifuge promptly to separate plasma/serum; avoid the buffy coat.
3. Mix 40 µl of plasma with 360 µl of extraction solution on dry ice.
4. Vortex, shake at 4°C, centrifuge, and filter using an Amicon Ultra-0.5 centrifugal filter unit.

E. Genomic NA Digestion Protocol:

1. Isolate genomic DNA using an appropriate kit (e.g., QIAGEN Neasy Blood & Tissue Kit).
2. Digest using Nucleoside Digestion Mix (NEB) following the manufacturer’s instructions.
3. Add water and methanol, vortex, centrifuge, and collect the supernatant for analysis.
